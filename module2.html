Skill Stacking
That Makes Sense

A single skill is a commodity. Anyone can do one thing. But a specific combination of skills — yours — creates a position that is difficult to replicate and nearly impossible to commoditize. This module turns your skill list into a strategic stack.

⏱ ~20 minutes

Lesson 2.1
Lesson 2.1 · Why Individual Skills Aren't Enough
The Difference Between Capability and Position
Lesson 2.1 — Individual Skills vs. Stacks
Voiceover · ~5 min · Add your audio file to enable playback
0:00

Here's the uncomfortable truth about skills: everyone has them. The market is full of people who can write, or manage projects, or analyze data, or design things, or talk to clients. A single skill — no matter how good you are at it — puts you in a category with thousands of other people.

What changes the equation is combination. When you layer skills together in a specific way — skills that support each other, amplify each other, and together solve a problem that no single skill can solve alone — you stop being a commodity and start being a category.

The difference: "I'm a writer" puts you in a category with millions. "I translate complex financial data into plain-language narratives for Series A founders who can't afford a full-time CFO" puts you in a category of one. That second description is a skill stack — writing plus financial literacy plus startup context plus communication design. No single skill gets you there.

Your job in this module is to find those combinations — the ones already buried in your experience from Module 1 — and name them deliberately. The Skill Stack Builder does exactly that.

Lesson 2.2
Lesson 2.2 · Identifying Your Natural Stack
Which Skills Were Always Working Together
Lesson 2.2 — Finding Your Natural Stack
Voiceover · ~5 min · Add your audio file to enable playback
0:00

Go back to your Harvest Matrix from Module 1. Look across the rows. You're not looking at individual skills — you're looking at which ones show up in the same row, or in adjacent rows solving the same type of problem. Those clusters are your natural stacks. They've been working together the whole time. You just hadn't named the combination yet.

Group your skills by function, not by job title. Communication skills, systems skills, execution skills, creative skills, analytical skills — look for which categories you pull from consistently and in combination. A person who is always pulling from both "systems" and "communication" every time they solve a problem has a systems-communication stack. That stack has a market.
