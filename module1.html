Skill Clarity
Without Job Titles

Most people underestimate their skills because they're looking at job titles — not capabilities. This module changes that. You're going to extract what you actually do, name the underlying skills, and see the patterns that have been running through your career all along.

Lesson 1.1
Lesson 1.1 · Skills vs. Titles
Why Your Resume Lied to You
Lesson 1.1 — Skills vs. Titles
Voiceover · ~5 min · Add your audio file to enable playback
0:00

A job title is a label your employer chose. It describes a role on an org chart — not what you actually did, and definitely not what you're capable of. When you introduce yourself by title, you're translating your entire skill set into the least specific version of yourself.

Skills are different. Skills are what you do repeatedly. They're the actions, decisions, and processes you've applied across multiple contexts — often without being asked, often without being compensated for, and almost always without being labeled as a skill.

Think about this: An "Operations Manager" who repeatedly identified communication gaps between teams and redesigned handoff processes that cut delays by 40% doesn't have a job title. They have a product waiting to happen. The title hides the skill. The skill is what someone pays for.

The Harvest Matrix you're about to complete is designed to get past the titles and surface what's actually there. Complete every row. Leave nothing blank.

Lesson 1.2
Lesson 1.2 · Pattern Recognition
Seeing What's Been There the Whole Time
Lesson 1.2 — Pattern Recognition
Voiceover · ~5 min · Add your audio file to enable playback
0:00

Your most valuable skills are not the most impressive ones. They're the ones that show up everywhere. The skill you've applied in three different industries, two different roles, and a volunteer situation is not a coincidence. It's a pattern — and patterns are products.

What to look for as you fill out the matrix: Which decisions did you make that no one asked you to make? Which problems did you solve before anyone acknowledged they were problems? What did people repeatedly rely on you for — even when it wasn't in your job description? Those answers are where the skills live.
🔒 Remember: This is documentation, not brainstorming. Complete every row. Vague entries don't count.

Three Diagnostic Questions
// Look Across Your Matrix Before Answering

Look at your completed rows. The patterns are in the repetition — not in any single role. Answer each question in writing.

1. Which 3 skills show up in almost every area of your life — not just one job or one environment?
What keeps appearing regardless of industry, role, or context? If you did it at work, at home, and in between — it counts.
2. Which of these skills solve problems people already pay for?
Not problems people theoretically have — problems with existing markets and payment history.
3. Which skill can you execute at a high level with the least effort?
Not the most impressive skill. The most executable one. Ease of execution is a competitive advantage.
