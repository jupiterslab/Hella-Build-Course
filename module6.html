The Generosity Trap — And How to Get Out of It
Lesson 6.1 — The Generosity Trap
Voiceover · ~5 min · Add audio file to enable playback
0:00

Here's the pattern. You're good at what you do. A client asks for something small — just a quick tweak, just one more revision, just a quick call outside of your normal structure. You say yes because you're capable and you want to help. Then it happens again. And again. Six weeks in, you've done 40% more work than you agreed to and you're resentful and exhausted. That's not a client problem. That's a structure problem.

Scope creep doesn't come from bad clients. It comes from undefined agreements. When the scope isn't written down, there is no scope — there's just a conversation that each party remembers differently. The client isn't testing you. They just don't know where the line is because you never drew it.

Structural boundaries vs. emotional ones: An emotional boundary is something you feel and enforce through willpower. A structural boundary is built into the agreement before the work starts. One requires you to fight for it every time. The other fights for itself.
Lesson 6.2
Lesson 6.2 · Designing for Long-Term Sustainability
Building Capacity, Not Just Hustle
Lesson 6.2 — Sustainability Over Hustle
Voiceover · ~5 min · Add audio file to enable playback
0:00

Most business advice tells you to work harder, take on more, and sleep less. That's not a business strategy — it's a burn rate. A sustainable business isn't one that works when you're running at 100%. It's one that still works when you're running at 60%.

Sustainability means you've designed your offers, your systems, and your client relationships to support your life — not replace it. It means knowing your capacity limit, protecting your off time the same way you protect your client time, and building income that doesn't require you to be in motion every single day.
